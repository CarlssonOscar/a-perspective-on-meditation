Meditation Tips

The hardest part about meditation is not the meditation, we become better at most things we put our minds to. The hardest part is to get a regular practice going.

Why do you want to meditate?

The first tip is, figure out why you want to meditate. As with most things, we don’t stick to things that we don’t do for a reason and meditation is not about reaching a goal and then quitting. Figure out why you would never want to stop. It may sound hard, but if you think about it most of us have habits we do every day, we shower and brush our teeth. I guess my commitment do meditation is quite similar to those habits. But I think of it as mental instead of physical hygiene.

Creating the habit

As with most habits it’s a good idea to set up your life in a way where it’s hard not to do the habit. I usually set an alarm with meditation music or a guided meditation instead of a regular alarm and put on a second alarm so I know when to stop. I know everybody can’t do this, but if you can, put your cellphone on airplane mode during the night. In the morning when you out of habit pull up your cellphone you may realize that your supposed to be meditating, not scrolling through Instagram. My experience is also that my mind is calmer in the morning if I have not started the day looking at my cellphone.

Write

Prior to the meditation, write down what you need to during the day. This will for many make it easier to go deeper in to the meditation faster.

You can manipulate your thoughts

Try stating ten or twenty things your truly thankful for in the begging of your meditation. You will notice that the mind will have more positive thoughts during the mediation, and sometimes through the day. This practice, at least for me, makes it easier to notice thoughts and can be a good way of understanding how meditation works. You may also realize that one of the reasons we don’t always have very positive thoughts is that we expose ourselves to lots of negativity, it’s everywhere. On the news, on social media, even the positive things on social media may make us jealous of the things we don’t have. It’s necessarily not a good idea to stop altogether with these things, it may make us ignorant, as will only sticking to one source of information about what happens in the world. But limiting your consumption of these things will likely make your thoughts more positive.

You don’t have to sit down to meditate

I think that when most people think of meditation they think of a person sitting with calming music in the background. But you don’t have to do this to notice thoughts. Several times during the day I notice that my mind is obsessing over something. When I do I usually go “Hi brain, what you up to”, and the go back to focusing on my breath. Than my mind usually calms down.

Listen to guided meditations

There are several people who hold guided meditations. My favorite is Tara Brach, you can find her on several music apps, she also has a podcast and have written several books about meditation and healing trauma. Sam Harris is another favorite. He has a meditation app called Waking Up, a podcast and have written several books about cognition. Both these people have talks about world problems, about human cognition and lots of other interesting topics. I think it’s important to expose ourselves to other people’s ideas. Just because we have an opinion about something doesn’t mean we are right. Remember that most minds hate change, and will often try fooling you in to believing that your right, even when your wrong.
