How To Meditate

Find a sitting position you’re comfortable with. Make sure your posture is upright, put your hands in your lap and relax. You can sit on a cushion, in a sofa or on a chair, with your legs crossed or firmly planted on the floor. Avoid lying down if you don’t want to fall asleep. If you want something in the background put on a meditation music list. Set a timer, you can start with five or ten minutes. You can increase the amount of time as you progress. I usually go between fifteen minutes up to an hour depending on what I want to accomplish.

If you want to be less distracted during the day, a shorter time is plenty for most. If you want to go deeper, a longer time is usually needed. In the beginning, lots of my thoughts are about what I’m going to do that day. Before I start I try to minimize this through writing down what I need to do after the meditation. As time passes my thoughts usually go to places of what I feel is of greater importance. It can be something I regret, something I try to repress, something I appreciate, something I feel I should do. From the research I have done over the years this seems to be a very common thought pattern.

It’s important to be kind to yourself and your thoughts, trying to avoid something usually doesn’t work. If you tell yourself to not think about a pink dinosaur with wings, you will inevitably think of that. A better approach is to observe the thought, say thank you for letting me know and then move on. If that does not work and the thought keeps coming up session after session, maybe you should investigate the thought, is it reasonable that I feel this way about what comes up? Mediation can help us observe thoughts instead of identifying with them. Sometimes it doesn’t make sense that we feel the way we feel about something that has happened to us, about something we did. If we realize this it can be easier to let go of the thought.

Sometimes it does make sense that we feel remorse, meditation can be a great moral compass. Lots of people, let’s face it, does some stupid shit during their lives, towards themselves and towards others. Apologizing to ourselves, or others you may have hurt, can be of great relief. Maybe you do not accept it at first, keeping trying you will get there. Maybe the people you hurt don’t accept your apology, at least you have done the thing you could do. We can’t change everything, but it’s likely a better approach to life to focus on what we can do, than being frustrated about what we can’t do.

I use several methods to mediate, the most common one is to focus on the breath. During meditation, your mind will wander multiple times, far away from the breath. When you notice that your mind is wandering, go back to the breath. With experience, you will get better at noticing thoughts arising. Thoughts arise randomly, sometimes it will be about something that does not matter. Sometimes you will hear your mind say that you should stop meditating. Sometimes it’s a pile of bullshit you tell yourself. Sometimes it can be something of importance. With time, it gets easier to tell the difference. Some thoughts are worth exploring, some you should let go and go back to your breath. A good way of knowing if your lost in thought or observing thoughts is to try to recollect what your thoughts were about; you might notice that it’s harder than it sounds.

Keep in mind that people meditate for different reasons, and there is no right way of doing it. For some, the only focus they have is to notice thoughts and coming back to the breath, bodily sensations or to silently go through a mantra. If one’s objective only is to become more present, this is the approach I would take. I do this quite a lot, and one can reach levels of presence that are hard to comprehend through this method. It’s hard to describe, but it sorts of feels like your detached from your body, your so focused that everything else than presence seem to vanish. I know it sound counter intuitive, but it can be a truly euphoric and calming experience at the same time.

Another method is to focus on bodily sensations. You may notice that your body is tense, if your stressed your jaw might be clenched Try scanning your body from head to toes and relax the parts of your body that are tense. Choose a body part to focus on. If you meditate with someone, hold their hand, the sensations that occur will be your area of focus.

The last method is looking at art. Pick a piece of art that you like and observe it. Thoughts and emotions will inevitably rise. The art may awake certain emotions depending on what it portrays and your interpretation of it. You may start analyzing what the artist had in mind when making it. It may remind you about certain things in your life. Whenever you notice that your attention has drifted away from the artwork, go back and watch it.

Meditation is a great tool for mental health. But don’t forget to talk about the road bumps you hit in your life. Needless if it’s with a friend or a psychologist it can be of great help to process what happens to you during life with someone else. Our mind often tells us why we are right and not why we are wrong, listening to other people’s ideas with an open mind can be of great help.
